OPDP Fair Balance Guidelines
============================

1. The Principle of Fair Balance
The Office of Prescription Drug Promotion (OPDP) requires that all promotional materials for prescription drugs present a "fair balance" between the benefits of the drug and its risks. 

2. Placement and Prominence
Risk information must be presented with a prominence and readability reasonably comparable to the presentation of the drug's benefits. It is unacceptable to bury risk information in footnotes, small font, or fast-scrolling text while benefits are presented in large, bold fonts or spoken slowly and clearly.

3. Audio and Visual Synchrony
In video advertisements (such as TV commercials or social media video ads):
- Major statement of risks must be presented in a clear, conspicuous, and neutral manner.
- Audio delivery of risk information must match the pacing and volume of the benefit information.
- Background music or visual distractions (e.g., loud upbeat music, dancing, rapid scene changes) must not interfere with the viewer's ability to comprehend the risk information being presented.
- If risks are presented visually (text on screen), the text must be on screen long enough to be read by the average viewer.

4. Comprehensiveness
The advertisement must disclose the most significant risks associated with the product, including any boxed warnings, contraindications, and major side effects. It is not necessary to list every single minor side effect in a consumer-facing ad, but the most severe and common risks must be present.

5. Comparative Risk
If an advertisement claims that a drug is safer or has fewer side effects than a competitor, this claim must be backed by substantial evidence, typically requiring head-to-head clinical trials. Implied comparative claims regarding safety are subject to the same strict substantiation requirements.
