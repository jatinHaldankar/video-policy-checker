WHO Ethical Criteria for Medicinal Drug Promotion
=================================================

1. Overview
The World Health Organization (WHO) provides ethical criteria to support the rational use of drugs. Promotional activities should be in keeping with national health policies and in compliance with national regulations, as well as voluntary standards where they exist. All promotion-making claims concerning medicinal drugs should be reliable, accurate, truthful, informative, balanced, up-to-date, capable of substantiation, and in good taste.

2. Avoidance of Misleading Information
Promotional materials must not contain misleading or unverifiable statements or omissions likely to induce medically unjustifiable drug use or give rise to undue risks. The word "safe" should only be used if properly qualified. Furthermore, it should not be stated that a product has no side effects, toxic hazards, or risks of addiction.

3. Advertising to the General Public
Advertisements directed to the general public should help people make rational decisions about the use of drugs available legally without a prescription (over-the-counter drugs). While these advertisements should account for peoples legitimate desire for information regarding their health, they should not take undue advantage of peoples concern for their health. Prescription drugs should not be advertised directly to the general public to prevent self-diagnosis and irrational usage.

4. Use of Symbology and Endorsements
Advertisements should not be directed at children. The use of symbols, figures, and endorsements from public figures or celebrities to inappropriately glamorize a drug is discouraged, as it detracts from the clinical and scientific basis for the drug's use. Claims that a drug is a "miracle cure" are explicitly prohibited.
